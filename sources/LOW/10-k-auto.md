## Item 1 — Business Description

Lowe's Companies, Inc. is a Fortune 50 company and the world's second largest home improvement retailer. As of January 31, 2025, the company operated **1,748 home improvement stores and outlets** in the United States.

Lowe's serves two primary customer groups:
- **DIY (Do-It-Yourself):** Homeowners who complete projects themselves
- **Pro (Professional):** Tradespeople, repair and remodel contractors, and property managers

The company's overarching strategy is called the **"Total Home Strategy"** — aimed at being the complete destination for home improvement needs across both DIY and Pro customers.

### Pro Segment Strategy
The Pro segment has become a key growth engine. Key elements include:
- Pro now accounts for approximately **40% of total revenue**, up from approximately 20-25% in 2023
- "Total Home Strategy" targets increasing Pro customer penetration from 19% (2019 baseline) toward 30%
- Pro loyalty program expanding to target plumbers, electricians, and HVAC contractors with dedicated services
- **Strategic acquisitions in FY2025**: FBM (130+ branches, focus on large-scale interior/exterior building products) and ADG (Ace Distribution Group) were acquired to serve **larger Pro customers** in residential and commercial markets
- FBM's network gives Lowe's access to drywall, HVAC systems, and other materials needed for bigger job sites — territory historically dominated by specialty distributors

## Item 1 — Competition (vs. Home Depot and Others)

Lowe's competes with a broad set of players:
- General merchandise retailers (e.g., Walmart, Target)
- Home goods specialty stores
- Warehouse clubs (Costco, Sam's Club)
- Online retailers (Amazon)
- Other specialty retailers
- Equipment and tool rental providers
- Service providers that install home improvement products
- Wholesalers serving homeowners, renters, businesses, and government

**Key competitive factors** (as stated in the 10-K):
1. Location of stores
2. Product assortment
3. Product pricing
4. Customer service
5. Omnichannel capabilities (technology, AI/machine learning, fulfillment)

**vs. Home Depot:** Lowe's is the explicit #2 in the industry. Home Depot has a stronger historical position with Pro/contractor customers. Lowe's "Total Home Strategy" and FBM/ADG acquisitions represent a direct effort to close the Pro customer gap with HD. In the DIY segment, both companies compete heavily on price, assortment, and store experience.

## Item 1A — Risk Factors (Key Excerpts)

### 1. Housing Market and Macroeconomic Risk
- Demand for Lowe's products is closely tied to housing turnover, home price appreciation, age of housing stock, and consumer confidence
- Higher interest rates have materially suppressed housing turnover — fewer home sales means fewer renovation triggers
- Consumer discretionary spending has been adversely impacted by inflationary pressures and higher rates

### 2. Tariff and Trade Risk
- Significant portion of merchandise is sourced from outside the US (particularly China and Asia)
- Increased tariff activity (2025-2026 environment) creates direct cost pressure on COGS
- Longer-term macro consequences of tariffs include: slower growth, inflation, and higher interest rates
- Lowe's ability to pass costs to consumers depends on competitive dynamics — competitors face the same pressures

### 3. Cybersecurity Risk
- Lowe's maintains a cybersecurity program to address security threats (details in Item 1C)
- As a large retailer with extensive customer payment data and supply chain integrations, cyber incidents could disrupt operations and harm reputation

### 4. Competition Risk
- Intense competition from Home Depot and other retailers
- Evolution of AI and e-commerce raises the stakes for omnichannel investment
- Failure to keep pace with technology (fulfillment, digital experience) could erode share

### 5. Operational and Supply Chain Risk
- Dependence on third-party suppliers and manufacturers
- Supply chain disruptions (geopolitical, logistics, weather) can impact product availability and margins
